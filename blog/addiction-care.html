Addiction Care

Mar 02, 2023

You meet someone special; You go through specific stages in your relationship, from the initial, nervous stages to the more committed stages.

Finally, you take the relationship to the next level, moving in together before making the more serious commitment of being engaged and married. Maybe you decide to start a family, maybe not.

But then, over time, the little things become big, you start fighting more often, there’s a lack of intimacy, and worse, a lack of interest in the other person.

Divorce is decided.

This description may sound short and sweet, but it’s more difficult for people than you realize. People have a hard time with change, especially major changes in their lives. Some treat it as trauma. Others struggle to find healthy ways to adapt and accept the new reality.

Sometimes people adopt healthy coping mechanisms, but other times, because Divorce can be stressful. And painfully, those coping strategies may be less helpful. People often turn to self-destructive behaviors such as alcohol or drug use as ways to get out of the fun they’ve had because of a divorce.

Infidelity and financial stress are the two most common reasons for divorce, although substance abuse is a close second.

The average age of Americans filing for divorce is 30 years old, and adults ages 25-34 are more prone to substance abuse than any other age group. Moreover, those who suffer from alcoholism or dependence have a much higher divorce rate than other groups.

Addiction affects many other areas of the sufferer’s life apart from their marriage.

  • Financial probleBenefit fms are frequent.
  • Dependency becomes a major priority in the victim’s life.
  • Emotional abuse and trauma have become more common.
  • Lack of trust and lack of communication make their relationship more strained.
  • Children of parents with addiction problems are more prone to drug or alcohol abuse.
  • Children of divorced families are more likely to divorce themselves.

It’s common for people going through a divorce to experience a range of emotions, from sadness to grief and even anger. gave The divorce process can be difficult.Complex, and overwhelming, that’s why it’s so important for people going through divorce to lean on their support groups and find healthy coping mechanisms.

Sometimes, however, relationships damaged by addiction and dependency can survive addiction, but this requires treatment and work for recovery.

In cases of divorce based on addiction issues, finding appropriate treatment options and treatments that will not only help the addiction but also provide a foundation for a healthy life by addressing the underlying issues related to addiction, can help both the relationship and the victim. Individuals will get help together.

A variety of treatments are available.

Finding addiction treatment can take different forms depending on the person’s addiction. Some may find help with traditional talk therapy and behavioral therapy, while others may need to seek medical attention. The key to therapy is recovery and long-term support against relapse.

Behavior therapy

The most common form of treatment and rehabilitation is behavioral in practice and design.

For example, in behavioral therapy, you may work on addictive triggers and develop coping strategies and skills to avoid the urge to use, either individually, in family, or in group settings. will do

There are a variety of behavioral therapies to choose from.

  • Exercise rehabilitation services – focusing on physical activity as a way to deal with stress and triggers that cause negative behaviors.
  • Christian Restoration Services -Focus on the ability to look to scripture for help in difficult times to create a path to deeper understanding
  • Hypnotherapy – focuses on changing the perception of addictive stimuli.
  • Art, music, and creative therapy – Emphasis on mental catharsis and providing an outlet for stress that leads to negative behaviors.

Medication Assisted Treatment (MAT)

In more severe cases of addiction, medication may be needed and is evaluated by a board-certified addiction specialist. In these cases, a MAT course is recommended, and non-addictive medications are prescribed to help with withdrawal and addiction-related cravings.

MATs stand for medication-assisted treatment and do not deal with the underlying cause of addiction, but treat physical and psychological dependence.
